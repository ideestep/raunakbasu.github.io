Research

Our lab aims to understand how the brain constructs internal representations of the surrounding world and how these representations support flexible, goal-directed decision-making. We take an interdisciplinary approach combining behavioral paradigms, in vivo electrophysiology, neural perturbation techniques, and computational analysis.

Our work is organized around three interconnected research themes.

Task-Relevant Cognitive Map Formation

Adaptive behavior requires more than a physical map of space. We investigate how neural circuits construct task-relevant cognitive maps that encode abstract variables such as value, context, and expected outcomes, and how these maps update when task contingencies change.

Neural Basis of Spatial Decision-Making

Spatial choices often rely on internally maintained representations rather than immediate sensory input. We study how representations of goals, spatial structure, and reward expectations interact to guide navigation and flexible decision-making.

Coordination Between Distributed Neural Maps

Flexible planning depends on coordinated activity across multiple brain regions. Our research focuses on how distributed spatial and non-spatial representations interact across neural circuits to enable navigation, inference, and adaptive behavior in dynamic environments.
