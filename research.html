Our lab aims to understand how the brain constructs internal representations of the surrounding world and how these representations support flexible, goal-directed decision-making. To address these questions, we take a highly interdisciplinary approach combining behavioral paradigms, in vivo electrophysiology, neural perturbation techniques, and computational analyses.

Broadly, our work is organized around three interconnected research directions.

Task-relevant cognitive map formation

Effective decision-making requires representations that extend beyond physical space. We investigate how the brain forms cognitive maps that integrate abstract variables such as value, cost, and context, and how these representations adapt to changing task demands.

Neural basis of spatial decisions

Spatial decisions often depend on information that is not immediately available to the senses. We examine how internal representations of space, goals, and outcomes are used to evaluate trade-offs and guide navigation and choice.

Coordination between distributed maps

Flexible behavior relies on interactions across multiple brain regions. Our work focuses on how distinct spatial and non-spatial maps are coordinated across neural circuits to support navigation and planning in complex, dynamic environments.
